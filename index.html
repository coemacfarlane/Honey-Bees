Jobs of the Hive

Every bee has a role to play in the hive. The Queen stays with the colony and reproduces with the drones. Worker bees maintain the hive and can be broken into two groups: House bees remain in the hive processing honey and taking care of larvae while forager bees leave to collect nectar.

All these bees work together to sustain the hive and make honey. The honey process begins with the forager bees who leave the hive to search for flowers.

Nectar and House Bees

House Bees

Back in the hive, the forager bee will give the collected nectar to the house bees who then store the honey.

Enzymes within the bees break down the sugars in the nectar as they pass it between each other. House bees continue passing the honey between each other until the water level is low enough to store it.

Love Your Local Bees

Bees don't just make honey â€” they're also important pollinators! Without bees' help we wouldn't have the beautiful plants or food we enjoy on a daily basis. They're an important part of almost every ecosystem but they're in danger.

Colony Collapse Disorder (CCD) has been threatening bee populations for years. This is when the worker bees abandon a queen and seemingly healthy hive. There may be a Queen and some nurse bees left but a hive cannot sustain itself without workers and will eventually die.

While the reported cases of incidence have slowed over the last five years, bee colonies are still being lost and overall bee health continues to suffer. CCD is horrible but it is not the only threat to bees. Pesticides, invasive species, alterations to habitat, inadequate forage and poor nutrition are all dangerous to honey bees and thus, by extension, our environment.

The causes of colony collapse may not be fully known, but the cause of most other above listed threats are. Farming, expanding suburbs, and growing cities have a massive toll on the environment as they take land that bees (and other animals) once lived on while utilizing massive amounts of chemicals.

You and I alone may not be able to change the affects man-kind has on nature, but organizing and educating yourself and your community on these issues can lead to great things. Even just starting a small garden of native species can have a bigger effect than you know.
